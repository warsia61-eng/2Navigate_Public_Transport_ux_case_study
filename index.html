Context

The problem with Indian public transport isn't the buses — it's the information

Millions ride DTC, BEST, and BMTC every day. But for anyone who isn't a regular — a student who just moved cities, a professional commuting to a new office, a non-Hindi speaker navigating Delhi — each journey is a guessing game. No real-time boards at most stops. Buses don't announce stops clearly. Route maps are outdated. And nobody to ask except a conductor who can barely be heard over the engine. This app was designed to be that missing layer of clarity.

Problem statement

"How might we help non-native commuters in Indian cities navigate government buses with confidence — so they can travel without fear of boarding the wrong bus, missing their stop, or being stranded mid-route?"

User understanding

Users don't struggle with routes — they struggle with uncertainty in the moment

Through field observations at Kashmere Gate, Majestic, and Dadar — and interviews with 18 commuters — four core anxieties emerged.

Research insights

What field research and data told us

  • 01Certainty about direction matters more than journey speed — users take longer routes if they're sure it's correct
  • 02Live bus position reduces stop-line anxiety — even approximate ETAs change behaviour significantly
  • 03Too many route options cause paralysis — users abandon when shown 6+ routes with no guidance
  • 04Language is a barrier — non-Hindi speakers struggle with stop names written only in Devanagari
  • 05Missed bus is the most distressing state — no fallback means users feel completely helpless
Research Board — Affinity Clusters
5 clusters surfaced from 18 interviews across Delhi, Mumbai, and Bangalore. Each cluster maps to a direct design decision.

Define

Users don't need more information.
They need the right information, at the right moment,
in a language they understand.

Strategy

Three pillars that shaped every design decision

Clarity

One recommended route, not six. Direction labels on every bus card. Reduce cognitive noise at every step.

Confidence

Live bus location, real ETAs with timestamps, and honest system states — even when things go wrong.

Guidance

Step-by-step journey mode with boarding alerts, stop countdowns, and bilingual stop names throughout.

Usability testing

Tested in the field — not just in a lab

Testing at Kashmere Gate ISBT (Delhi), Majestic Bus Stand (Bangalore), and Dadar TT Circle (Mumbai) with 12 participants — 6 of whom were new to the city.

Tested for

Route selection speed
ETA readability in sunlight
Stop alert timing accuracy
Edge case discoverability

Changes made

Reduced visible routes from 6 to 2 + "View more"
Increased ETA size for outdoor readability
Moved "Get ready" alert to 2 stops before
Redesigned missed bus flow entirely

Results

18s → 7s

Route selection time reduced from ~18 seconds to ~7 seconds

5 / 6

New users boarded correct bus (up from 2/6 before redesign)

3 → 0

Missed stops reduced from 3 to 0 in the final test round

↑ Conf.

Users reported feeling significantly more confident during travel

Edge cases

Designing for real-world scenarios

Real commuting doesn't follow the happy path. These four scenarios were designed explicitly — not treated as afterthoughts.

Missed bus
Bus missed

User was still walking when DTC 534 departed from Kashmere Gate.

→ "You missed this bus. Next DTC 534 in 12 mins from the same stop." Option to reroute via DTC 318.

Wrong direction
Off-route

User boarded towards Anand Vihar instead of Mehrauli. Journey Mode detects the mismatch.

→ "This bus is going the opposite way. Get off at next stop and catch DTC 318 from the other side."

No internet
Offline mode

User enters a tunnel or low-coverage zone mid-journey.

→ "You're offline — showing last known routes." Cached journey continues. Live tracking resumes when signal returns.

Location drift
Off-route user

User's GPS drifts or they walked away from their planned route.

→ "Looks like you moved — want to update your route from here?" Re-anchors without interrupting the journey.

"Designed for real-world uncertainty, not ideal conditions."

Learnings

What this project taught me

Designing for messy, real-world conditions is harder than designing for ideal use cases — and more important.
Simplicity isn't removing features. It's making the right feature impossible to miss.
Microcopy is UX. "Running 8 mins late near ITO" builds more trust than "Bus delayed" ever could.
Edge cases are just overlooked users. Designing for missed buses and offline states is designing for dignity.
